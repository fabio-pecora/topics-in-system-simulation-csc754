Project 1: Survey Paper
Agent Modeling and Simulation:
Agent modeling and simulation is an area of research that helps us understand how autonomous agents, whether they‚Äôre people, organizations, or artificial entities, interact within complex systems. By simulating individual agents with their own decision-making processes, behaviors, and learning mechanisms, we can analyze how these interactions shape larger patterns and outcomes. This approach is widely used in artificial intelligence, economics, robotics, healthcare, and even social sciences to model everything from traffic systems to financial markets. With advancements in machine learning, multi-agent systems, and high-performance computing, simulations today are more detailed and dynamic than ever. Researchers use agent-based modeling to study real-world problems like epidemic outbreaks, supply chain disruptions, and smart city planning. As these techniques evolve, they continue to offer new insights into decision-making, optimization, and predictive analysis across different industries. For this survey, we‚Äôll explore key foundational research, recent innovations, and major applications in agent modeling and simulation. By reviewing and categorizing important papers, we hope to provide a clear picture of where this field stands today and where it‚Äôs headed in the future.

  2. Alireza Kabirian, &Sigurdur Olafsson (2009). SIMULATION OPTIMIZATION WITH HYBRID GOLDEN REGION SEARCH. In Proceedings of the 2009 Winter Simulation Conference. Retrieved from INFORMS Simulation Society.
    3. Comment:
    This paper introduces the Golden Region (GR) search, a new method for solving simulation optimization problems where the objective function is expensive or difficult to evaluate directly. The method is built on the idea of dividing the feasible region into smaller sub-regions and using a smart strategy to decide which region to search next. In each step, GR considers how much a region has been explored (space score), how good the results in that region have been so far (quality score), and predictions from a trained model (metamodel score). These scores help the algorithm focus its search where the best results are likely to be found, balancing exploration of new areas and refinement of promising ones. The paper also explains how the method adapts over time by updating the importance of the three criteria based on their past success. The authors show that the GR method fits within a larger class of algorithms that are proven to converge to the global optimum under certain conditions. They test GR on benchmark problems and compare it to other well-known methods like Genetic Algorithms and Simulated Annealing. The results suggest that GR is efficient and performs well, even without heavy parameter tuning.
